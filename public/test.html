Your visceral fat of 185g is within the safe range (<250g). This indicates healthy levels of internal abdominal fat. Continue maintaining your current lifestyle and exercise routine.

Types of fat

Essential fat is found in your brain, bone marrow, and nerves. Subcutaneous fat is stored under the skin — it’s the fat you can squeeze. Visceral fat surrounds abdominal organs like the liver, kidneys, and heart.

Harmful effects

Excess visceral fat is linked to Type-2 diabetes, heart attacks, stroke, raised blood pressure, Alzheimer’s disease, and colorectal cancer. It’s more dangerous than subcutaneous fat.

How to reduce it

  • Reduce carb & sugar intake
  • Avoid processed foods & trans-fat
  • Reduce alcohol intake
  • Regular cardio & resistance training
  • Consume more protein
  • Improve sleep quality

What is T-Score & Z-Score?

T-Score compares your bone mass to a healthy young adult. Z-Score compares to others your age. A T-Score below -2.5 indicates osteoporosis.

When to be concerned?

T-Score below -1 indicates osteopenia (weaker bones). Below -2.5 is osteoporosis. Consult a doctor if your scores are low.

How to improve?

  • Calcium & Vitamin D
  • Weight-bearing exercise
  • Avoid smoking & excess alcohol
  • Resistance training

Also known as Waist to Hip Ratio

What is A/G Ratio?

The ratio of fat percentage in your Android (belly) region to your Gynoid (hip) region. It uses fat % instead of inches for accuracy, since someone with more muscle may still have a wider waist.

What is its significance?

A ratio below 1.0 is ideal — it means you carry less fat around your belly than your hips. Values above 1.0 correlate with higher visceral fat and metabolic risk. Your goal is to bring this below 1.0.

Less than 1.0

Associated with pear, hourglass body shapes. Lower risk of metabolic syndrome and cardiovascular disease. This is the desirable range.

Greater than 1.0

Associated with apple, rectangular body shapes. Higher prevalence of visceral fat. Focus on reducing abdominal fat through diet and core-focused training.

What are Macros?

Short for macronutrients — the three categories of nutrients you eat the most: carbohydrates, fats, and protein. They provide most of your energy.

Why is it important?

Counting macros helps you understand where your calories come from and how they affect your body. Not all calories are created equal.

Is it worth counting?

Yes — it helps you stay full, increase fat loss, and increase gains in lean mass. But also focus on vitamins, minerals, and whole foods.

What’s a good RMR?

There is no “good” RMR — it’s unique to your body. However, some people may be interested in increasing their RMR to burn more calories per day.

Can I increase RMR?

Building muscle is most effective long term. Other methods:

  • Move often
  • More whole foods
  • Eat enough & frequently
  • Interval training
  • Cryotherapy or ice bath
  • Caffeine

What is RMR vs BMR?

Both tests rely on indirect calorimetry. The protocol for BMR testing is much more restrictive. Measurements are taken in a dark room after 12 hours of fasting and 8 hours of sleep.
